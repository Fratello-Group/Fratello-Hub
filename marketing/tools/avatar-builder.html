The framework, so it sticks

The Core 5

The whole avatar framework, each piece given a picture you won’t forget. Reload it before you write.

The one law

You target an avatar in every ad whether you mean to or not. The only question is whether yours is sharp enough that a stranger thinks “that’s exactly me.”

Relatability → trust → purchase.

Everything below is just five ways to get sharper.

The shape of it

Experience is the trunk. Desire, Emotion and Behavior are branches off it. Demographic is a small graft near the top. One real-world event grows wildly different humans depending on which branch you follow.

Name the trunk alone = broad. Stack branches on top, on purpose, after = specific. Core Avatar = one category. Sub Avatar = two-to-four stacked, where the money is.

1

Desire

the engine

The picture. The current under the surface. On top is the specific want — “I want a decaf that actually tastes like coffee.” Underneath runs the deep current: Health, Comfort, Control, Status. Build the ad on the surface — it’s visible and specific enough for a stranger to recognize. The deep current is real but invisible; everyone wants comfort, so naming it singles out no one.

Why it’s the engine. It’s the only category already pointed at a purchase. Experience, emotion and behavior describe a state; desire describes a direction. Start the engine here.

The trap. Reaching for the deep current because it feels profound. “I want to be healthy” is wallpaper — too broad to move anyone. And never stack two desires; two directions tear the avatar in half.

Fratello cut. “I want proof of when my coffee was roasted” makes a roast-date checker sit up. “I want good coffee” could be anyone holding a mug.

Check — can you say it as “I want X / I need Y”? If not, it’s not a desire.
2

Experience

the trunk

The picture. The security-camera fact — whatever a lens pointed at the person would actually have recorded. The bag arrived four months stale. The grocery beans tasted flat. A camera can’t film a want or a feeling, so the moment you’re describing something the lens couldn’t capture, you’ve drifted into another category. “The bag was stale” is filmable; “hates that the bag was stale” is not.

Why you isolate it. It’s the only category that’s concrete and widely shared at once. Desires are shared but vague; emotions and behaviors are concrete but fracture people into small groups. The event itself is the widest concrete thing a whole crowd of strangers can nod at simultaneously.

The trap. Welding a branch on too early. “Frustrated about the stale bag” fuses two categories — now you only talk to the frustrated ones. Keep the trunk clean and one event forks into three humans: the betrayed, the resigned, and the one who just shrugged and went back to grocery beans. Sub-trap: for product experiences the outcome is part of the fact — “tried it and it didn’t work” is filmable; the disappointment is a branch.

Fratello cut. “Ordered ‘fresh’ specialty online and it showed up roasted four months ago” — one hook catches all three before you narrow.

Check — a circumstance or event, with the feeling stripped out.
3

Emotion

the branch that bypasses logic

The picture. Smoke and fire. The smoke — the basic emotions — is universal; everyone recognizes them. But smoke alone is generic. Relatability lives in tracing it back to this person’s specific fire, the trigger. Name the smoke (“you feel frustrated”) and it’s wallpaper. Name the fire (“the ‘premium’ bag made you look like a sucker”) and they feel read.

Why it’s powerful. Emotion bypasses logic. It gets someone to act before the rational “do I actually need this” filter kicks in. Universal emotion, individual trigger — that gap is the gold.

The trap. Stopping at the secondary emotion. Skeptical, anxious, overwhelmed are labels the brain slaps on to tidy a feeling — a sign to dig, not a destination. Ask why until you hit a basic emotion plus its trigger.

Fratello cut. “Skeptical of online coffee” is the smoke. Dig: it’s fear — they’ve been burned and don’t trust the freshness claim. Name the fear and its source, and the skeptic stops scrolling.

Check — does it describe how they feel in response to something? If it’s secondary, ask why until you hit a primary.
4

Behavior

the sharpest lever

The picture. The fingerprint — the visible mark desire, experience and emotion leave when they dry into a repeated action. You can’t see someone’s want, but you can watch them check the roast date every single time. The internal stuff, made visible and repeatable.

Why it’s the sharpest. It’s tied to ego — it’s who they are, not just what they do — so naming it reads as recognition, not a pitch. “If you grind fresh every morning…” isn’t selling; it’s a mirror. And it’s predictable: know the behavior and you can tell them their own next move.

The trap. Mistaking a one-off for a pattern. “Bought specialty once” is an experience; “grinds fresh every morning” is a behavior — the strength is in the frequency. And behaviors can be mental: “constantly overthinks which bag to buy” counts, and most people forget to hunt for the invisible ones.

Fratello cut. “Won’t buy beans that go oily because they’ll clog the machine” — that one behavior says they own a real setup, they’ve been burned, they care. The ad that names it doesn’t sell. It recognizes.

Check — something they do (physical or mental), and roughly how often. More often = stronger.
5

Demographic

last, and usually skippable

The picture. The fence, not the magnet. The other four are magnets — they pull people in by relatability. A demographic only decides who’s allowed inside and who’s locked out. Fences don’t make anyone want to come in.

When it earns its place. Only when the product genuinely serves that group better — then the fence turns into relatability. Decaf for the caffeine-sensitive; socks built for construction workers. Used that way, the fence itself says “this was made for you.”

The trap. Starting here — the single most common mistake there is. A 22-year-old woman and a 54-year-old man can share the same desire, experience, emotion and behavior. Lead with a demographic and you’ve torched half your market for the variable that moves purchase intent least.

Fratello cut. “Calgarians over 50” as a starting point is weak and alienating. But target “won’t buy oily beans that clog my machine” and the espresso-setup demographic rides along behind the behavior. The one place it genuinely leads for us: decaf.

Check — standard bucket (gender, age, location, employment, income)? And does the product actually serve them better? If not, skip.
Core Avatar

One category, alone. Wide reach — good for cold, top-of-funnel.

Sub Avatar

Two-to-four categories stacked into one sentence. Specific, uncomfortable, that’s-me — and where the money is.

The one move when you’re stuck

Start at the trunk. Name a clean security-camera fact a real customer lived (“ordered ‘fresh’ coffee, it showed up months stale”). Then walk it out the branches — what do they want underneath, how do they feel, what do they now do. You’ll have a full avatar and three or four ad angles before your coffee’s cold. Stack two-to-four branches into one sentence — that’s your Sub Avatar.
