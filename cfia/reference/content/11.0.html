Hazard Analysis and Preventive Controls for Incoming Materials (PCP Section 11)

Fratello Coffee Roasters conducts a comprehensive hazard analysis for all incoming materials in accordance with the Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR) and CFIA guidelines. SFCR 47(1) requires that we "identify and analyze the biological, chemical and physical hazards that present a risk of contamination of a food". SFCR 47(2) further mandates that we "prevent, eliminate or reduce to an acceptable level" those hazards through effective control measures. The following tables identify potential biological, chemical, and physical hazards for each product category (green coffee, tea, packaging, oat milk, chai concentrate, chocolate sauce, and flavored syrups), along with an assessment of likelihood, severity, overall risk rating, and appropriate preventive control measures. This hazard analysis aligns with CFIA's Preventive Control Plan (PCP) sub-elements 1.1(a) (Incoming inputs), 1.1(b/c) (Product formulation/processing), and 1.3 (Import controls). Critical Control Points (CCPs) are clearly designated where applicable.

Green Coffee (Raw Coffee Beans)

Green coffee beans can introduce various hazards into the facility. Biological hazards include pathogenic bacteria (e.g. Salmonella spp.) which may be present on raw beans from farm or drying environments. Molds can also grow on beans in humid conditions, producing chemical hazards like ochratoxin A (OTA), a mycotoxin. Potential physical hazards include foreign matter such as stones or other crop debris often found in bulk coffee shipments. Although roasting greatly reduces microbial risks (it is a kill step for pathogens), these incoming hazards must be identified and controlled to prevent contamination of the production environment and finished product.

Bulk Loose Leaf Tea

Bulk teas (black, green, herbal, etc.) can carry similar hazards as coffee since they are agricultural products. Biological hazards include contamination by pathogens such as Salmonella – in fact, there have been instances of Salmonella in dried teas leading to recalls. However, brewing tea with boiling water by the consumer typically acts as a kill step for microbes, reducing their risk in the final infusion. Chemical hazards are a concern with tea: notably, pesticide residues and heavy metals. CFIA targeted surveys have found a significant percentage of imported teas with pesticide residues above allowable limits (e.g. 41% of samples in one survey exceeded the general 0.1 ppm limit, with oolong tea having the highest violation rate). Tea plants can also accumulate metals from soil (e.g. aluminum, lead), though levels detected are usually comparable to literature and not acutely hazardous. Physical hazards in loose tea may include stems, strings, or foreign debris introduced during harvesting and drying.

These are considered Preventive Controls under our PCP rather than critical control points on our premises.

Packaging Materials (Boxes, Bags, Supplies)

Packaging materials (e.g. coffee bags, tea pouches, labels, corrugated boxes, etc.) are inputs that come into direct or indirect contact with food products. While packaging is inert, it can pose chemical and physical hazards if not food-grade or if contaminated. Chemical hazards include potential migration of harmful substances (inks, adhesives, plastic monomers) into food if the packaging is not compliant with food-grade standards. For example, non-food-grade inks or recycled fibers could leach chemicals. Physical hazards could arise from packaging components or damage – e.g. glass splinters from bottles/jars, metal staples or nails from packaging, or even wood slivers from pallet wood. Biological hazards are less direct, but packaging can be a vehicle for pests or filth: for instance, dirty boxes or bags could carry insect residues or rodent droppings if stored improperly. These hazards must be identified even though packaging is not ingested, because they can contaminate the food during packing.

Oat Milk (Dairy-Alternative Beverage)

Oat milk is a liquid food ingredient used in our cafe operations (e.g. for barista drinks) and possibly in product development. We typically receive oat milk in sealed cartons (often aseptic Tetra Pak or similar) that are shelf-stable until opened. Biological hazards include microbial spoilage or pathogen growth if the product is temperature-abused or if seal integrity fails. For instance, if a carton is damaged and not truly sterile, mold or bacteria could proliferate. Once opened, oat milk (especially if not UHT shelf-stable) is perishable and can support growth of microbes like Bacillus cereus (which can survive pasteurization as spores and multiply if left at room temp). Chemical hazards for oat milk are relatively limited: one consideration is allergens – while oats themselves are not a priority allergen in Canada, cross-contamination with gluten (wheat or barley) is common in oats and can be harmful to celiac consumers (gluten is a regulated hazard requiring precautionary labeling if present). Another chemical hazard could be undeclared sulfites or additives, though this is unlikely with reputable brands. Physical hazards might include packaging-related foreign objects (e.g. plastic cap pieces or Tetra Pak fragments if the container is cut open improperly). Below is the hazard assessment for oat milk as an incoming ingredient:

Chai Concentrate (Liquid Beverage Concentrate)

Chai concentrate is a brewed spice and tea concentrate (often sweetened) used for making chai lattes. It typically comes in sealed bottles or cartons. Many chai concentrates are acidified or shelf-stable products, but if the pH and processing are not properly controlled, they could support Clostridium botulinum (a deadly toxin-forming bacterium) – this is a serious biological hazard for any low-acid bottled beverage. (For example, a 2022 recall occurred in the US for an improperly acidified chai concentrate that posed a botulism risk.) However, reputable commercial chai concentrates are formulated to be either sufficiently acidic (pH <4.6) or are kept refrigerated to prevent this hazard. Other biological hazards include mold or yeast growth if the product is stored for long after opening (high sugar content slows growth, but surface mold can occur). Chemical hazards might include allergen presence – some chai concentrates may contain ingredients like honey or almond flavor (allergen risk if nuts are used, though uncommon). Also, sulfites could be used as preservatives in some formulas; >10 ppm sulfites would require declaration and can cause reactions in sensitive individuals. Physical hazards are possible from packaging (glass bottle breakage is a key concern if the chai concentrate is in glass) or from spice sediments (not harmful, but could be perceived as foreign if large). Chocolate Sauce (Flavoring Syrup/Sauce for Beverages)

Chocolate sauce is a sweet, viscous syrup used for mochas and other flavored coffees. We typically use commercially prepared chocolate sauce (e.g. in squeeze bottles or pouches). Key hazards include allergens and spoilage. Many chocolate sauces contain milk (if it's a milk chocolate or contains dairy ingredients like whey or condensed milk) – milk is a priority allergen in Canada and must be declared/controlled. Some also contain soy (soy lecithin as an emulsifier) which is another common allergen. Thus an allergen (chemical hazard) assessment is critical: using a chocolate sauce that contains milk or soy could introduce allergen risk to consumers if not handled properly (e.g. cross-contact with non-allergen products, or failing to declare it in any compounded product). Biological hazards: chocolate sauce is high in sugar which makes water activity low, so harmful bacterial growth is inhibited; however, molds or osmophilic yeasts might grow on sauce residue around container openings if not kept clean. There's a minor risk of bacterial contamination if the sauce is not properly processed, but commercial sauces are usually pasteurized. Chemical hazards beyond allergens are minimal (perhaps quality issues like rancidity if oils are used, but not food safety per se). Physical hazards are unlikely since the sauce is homogeneous; however, if packaged in glass jars, glass breakage would be a hazard (our current chocolate sauce comes in plastic bottles, so low risk there). Flavored Syrups in Glass Bottles

Flavored syrups (such as vanilla, caramel, hazelnut, etc.) are used for coffee flavoring. They are typically high-sugar liquids packed in glass bottles. Physical hazards are a major focus here because of the glass packaging – glass breakage could introduce shards into the syrup, which is a serious risk. Each glass bottle represents a potential physical hazard if mishandled. We also consider biological hazards: these syrups have high sugar and often preservatives, so they rarely support bacterial growth, but as with chocolate sauce, mold can grow at the bottle opening or in the pump if left unclean. Chemical hazards for flavored syrups include allergens if any of the flavors are derived from allergenic sources. Most common syrups are made with artificial or non-allergenic natural flavors, but there are notable exceptions (e.g. an "almond" or "hazelnut" syrup might contain real nut extract or essence – some Torani® hazelnut syrup has hazelnut allergen). Also, some caramel-type syrups may contain milk derivatives for richness (though usually they're non-dairy unless labeled otherwise). We must ensure any allergen in these syrups is identified and controlled. Standard Operating Procedure: Hazard Identification & Control of Incoming Materials (Section 11)

Purpose: This SOP defines the process for identifying food safety hazards in incoming materials and implementing preventive control measures, in compliance with SFCR 47(1) and 47(2) and CFIA PCP requirements. It ensures all raw materials, ingredients, and packaging used by Fratello Coffee Roasters are assessed for biological, chemical, and physical hazards, and that effective controls (including CCPs where applicable) are in place to prevent contamination of products. This aligns with PCP sub-element 1.1(a) (Incoming inputs hazard analysis), as well as 1.1(b/c) (hazards in product formulation/processing steps) and 1.3 (controls for imported inputs).

Scope: This procedure covers all incoming materials used in our production and cafe operations, including green coffee beans, loose leaf teas, packaging materials, oat milk, chai concentrates, chocolate sauces, flavored syrups, and any other ingredients. It begins at sourcing/purchasing and continues through receiving, storage, and initial usage of these inputs. It applies to the Quality Assurance team for hazard analysis and to all employees involved in receiving and handling ingredients.

Responsibility:

  • The Quality Assurance (QA) Manager is responsible for maintaining the hazard analysis for all inputs, updating hazard assessments whenever a new material is sourced or a change occurs, and verifying that control measures are implemented.
  • The Purchasing/Supply Chain Manager ensures that all suppliers are approved and that necessary documentation (COAs, guarantees, specifications) is obtained and reviewed for safety criteria.
  • Receiving personnel are responsible for conducting incoming inspections as per this SOP and reporting any deviations (e.g. damaged packaging, signs of contamination).
  • The Production Manager/Cafe Manager ensures staff follow handling and storage controls outlined here (e.g. temperature controls, allergen segregation).

Procedure

Hazard Identification (Pre-Purchase): For each material considered for use, QA conducts a hazard identification exercise: list all potential biological, chemical, and physical hazards associated with the material before any control is applied. This includes reviewing sources of the ingredient (e.g. farm environment for coffee/tea – pathogens, pests; processing steps – introduction of allergens or chemicals; packaging – physical hazards from materials). A hazard analysis worksheet or table is filled out for the new item, similar to the tables above, referencing known hazards (e.g. CFIA, Health Canada data on that commodity's hazards).

Supplier Approval & Specifications: Prior to ordering, ensure the supplier is approved as per our Supplier Approval Program. Obtain and review specifications and safety documents for the material: for example, ingredient spec sheets, Certificates of Analysis (for microbiological criteria, mycotoxins, etc.), allergen declarations, and, if relevant, Letters of Guarantee for packaging materials. Check these against known hazards:

  • Microbiological criteria: e.g. coffee/tea should have Salmonella "negative/25g". Oat milk should be pasteurized/UHT with an acceptable total plate count. Chai concentrate pH should be in safe range. Verify any COA results meet our standards.
  • Chemical criteria: e.g. pesticide residues in tea/coffee within limits; mycotoxins (OTA) in coffee below acceptable level; no undeclared additives. Ensure packaging complies with food-grade regulations (e.g. FDR Division 23).
  • Allergens: Confirm if the material contains any priority allergens or if there is a risk of cross-contact. (Use the CFIA list of priority food allergens as a reference.) Update our allergen control plan if a new allergen is introduced.
  • Physical specifications: e.g. "free of foreign matter" clauses or screening steps the supplier takes (like metal detection, sieving). If not explicitly stated, we may request information on how the supplier controls physical hazards.

Incoming Receiving Checks (Hazard Prevention at receiving): Upon arrival of materials, the receiving staff perform documented checks to catch any hazards:

  • Visual Inspection: Examine the delivery vehicle and packaging for cleanliness and integrity. Reject shipment if pests, excessive dirt, or chemical spills are evident in the truck or containers (to avoid contamination). Ensure seals are intact where applicable.
  • Material Inspection: For each item:
    • Check packaging for damage. No leaks, tears, broken seals or broken glass should be present. If found, isolate and assess the material for contamination.
    • Verify label and lot codes match what was ordered, including allergen info. Ensure allergen-containing items are clearly identified and segregated upon receipt.
    • Temperature-check perishable items (e.g. oat milk if refrigerated) and record the temperature. Accept only if within required range (e.g. ≤4°C for chilled items).
    • Organoleptic check (sight, smell) for any signs of spoilage or contamination (off-odors that could indicate chemical or microbial issues).
    • For raw materials like green coffee or tea: do a quick visual scan of contents (where possible) for foreign objects (stones, etc.) or obvious mold. Use sieves or black trays to help spot foreign matter or insect presence.
  • Document the receiving inspection on the Incoming Materials Checklist form. Note any deviations and action taken (e.g. "one bag had rodent droppings – rejected and segregated, supplier notified").

Hazard Evaluation and Risk Rating: QA evaluates any hazard observations or lab results to decide on acceptance and control actions. This includes determining the likelihood and severity of identified hazards in that lot:

  • If a hazard is present at an unsafe level (e.g. positive pathogen test, allergen cross-contact, pesticide above MRL), the lot is rejected or held and the supplier is contacted – this is a "significant hazard" that cannot be accepted.
  • If minor hazards are noted (e.g. a few small foreign pieces within spec limits), the material may be accepted but flagged for additional sorting or processing controls (and supplier feedback given).
  • Each hazard's risk is assessed to update our risk rating if needed. QA uses this information to update the hazard analysis tables and determine if our current controls are sufficient or if new controls/CCPs are needed. For example, if a new hazard emerges (say a supplier warns of peanut contamination in coffee), we would evaluate severity (High) and likelihood (Very low) and might implement additional controls (like allergen testing, or changing supplier).

Preventive Control Measures Implementation: For each significant hazard identified (those rated high risk or not acceptable by law), implement control measures to prevent, eliminate or reduce the hazard to an acceptable level. This can include:

  • Process Controls (CCPs): If the hazard will be controlled by our processing, designate a CCP with a critical limit. For instance, for green coffee pathogen risk, the roasting step is assigned as a CCP with a critical limit of X°C for Y minutes (validated to achieve 5-log reduction of Salmonella). Monitor each roast batch log for compliance and verify quarterly with calibration and perhaps occasional microbiological testing. Include this CCP in the roasting SOP with clear corrective actions if roast parameters fall short (e.g. re-roast or discard under-roasted coffee).
  • If a hazard could be controlled by a CCP but we lack one at that step, modify the process or move control upstream. (As CFIA notes, if no control measure exists for a significant hazard, we must adjust the process or product to address it.)
  • Supply Chain Controls: Many hazards in our ingredients are controlled at the supplier level. Maintain robust supplier controls as preventive measures. This includes supplier audits or requiring GFSI certification, routine review of supplier food safety performance, and specifying COA requirements. For example, require each lot of chai concentrate to have a pH certificate, or each lot of tea to have a pesticide test report. These controls are documented as part of our preventive control plan (PCP) under incoming materials.
  • Prerequisite Programs: Implement or reference relevant GMP programs as controls for hazards: e.g. Sanitation and Pest Control Program to prevent pest contamination of packaging, Allergen Control Program to prevent cross-contact, Storage and Temperature Control SOPs for refrigerated items. These are preventive measures that address hazards not at a CCP step but continuously (per SFCR Division 4 requirements). Ensure these programs are effective (verified via internal audits and records).

Critical Control Points (CCP) Management: For any CCP identified (currently, the roasting step is a CCP for biological hazards in coffee), follow the HACCP principles:

  • Monitor CCPs at the required frequency (e.g. every roast batch log reviewed for time/temperature).
  • Keep records of CCP monitoring (roast log, metal detector log if one is used in future, etc.).
  • Establish corrective actions for CCP deviations (e.g. if roast temp was insufficient, quarantine that batch, re-process if possible or destroy, and record the incident).
  • Verify CCPs regularly – calibration of roaster temp probes, validation of the roast profile's efficacy (e.g. review literature or test that our profile achieves desired lethality).
  • Include CCP review in internal audits to ensure no critical step is missed or mis-monitored.

Documentation: Maintain a Hazard Analysis Table or log for each product/category (as shown in the earlier sections) as part of Section 11 of the Preventive Control Plan binder. This document should detail for each ingredient/input: the identified hazards, their analysis (likelihood, severity, risk level), and the control measures in place. Justify any determination of a hazard being "not significant" or not likely (for transparency). Also, document the rationale for controls chosen. For example, note that "Salmonella in coffee is controlled by roasting (CCP) and further ensured by brewing by end-user" – this demonstrates we've analyzed and addressed that hazard.

  • Keep on file all supplier-provided documents (COAs, guarantees, etc.) and incoming inspection records as evidence of control. These are part of our PCP records to show compliance.
  • Any time a new hazard emerges or an incident occurs (e.g. a recall of a syrup due to allergen or a contamination event), update the hazard analysis document and this SOP if needed.

Training and Communication: Ensure all relevant staff are trained on the hazards associated with the materials they handle and the control measures in place. For instance, receiving staff training will cover what hazards to check during intake (they should know, for example, that loose tea could have Salmonella – so they handle it carefully and follow our inspection protocol). Likewise, production staff should understand why we segregate allergens or why roasting logs are critical. Maintain training records and include hazard awareness in refresher trainings.

Verification and Review: The Food Safety Team (led by QA) will review the entire hazard analysis and this Section 11 SOP at least annually or whenever there is a significant change (new product, new supplier, new hazard info). This review will verify that:

  • No new hazards have arisen or, if they have, they are now included.
  • Control measures are working effectively (review monitoring records, product testing results, customer complaints for any trend of hazard).
  • The risk ratings are still accurate given current data (for example, if new research shows a higher risk of OTA in coffee from a certain region, we might adjust likelihood or require more testing).
  • CCPs, if any, are properly controlled and effective (validate as needed).
  • The procedure remains aligned with current CFIA regulations and guidance. (Check CFIA updates on SFCR or guidance yearly.)

Any findings from verification (e.g. a gap in control, or an opportunity to improve) will result in corrective actions or updates to the PCP. We document the review and any amendments made to this SOP or the hazard tables.

References

  • Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR) Part 4, Division 2 – Biological, Chemical and Physical Hazards (sections 47(1) and 47(2)).
  • CFIA Preventive Control Plan Guidelines – in particular, PCP generic model sub-element 1.1a (Incoming inputs), 1.1b/1.1c (Hazard analysis of formulation and processing), 1.3 (Import controls) expectations.
  • CFIA Guidance "Conducting a Hazard Analysis" – used for methodology of evaluating likelihood and severity of hazards.
  • Health Canada and CFIA resources for specific hazards (e.g. CFIA targeted survey reports on Ochratoxin A, pesticide residues in tea, etc.), and the CFIA Reference Database for Accepted Packaging Materials for packaging safety.
  • Fratello Coffee Roasters Internal Documents: Supplier Assurance Program, Allergen Control Plan, Sanitation & Pest Control SOP, Temperature Control Log, Roast Process CCP Record.

This SOP (Section 11 of the Preventive Control Plan binder) ensures that our team systematically identifies hazards in all inputs and implements control measures verified by evidence, meeting the CFIA's food safety requirements. By following this procedure, we maintain our commitment to producing safe, high-quality coffee products and related beverages, protecting our customers and complying with Canadian food safety regulations.
