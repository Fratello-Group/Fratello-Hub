CFIA Expectations for Mock Recalls

Under the Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR), every food business must conduct a recall simulation at least once every 12 months and document the details and results of that mock recall. This is a legal requirement to verify your recall readiness. The written record of the simulation must be kept on file for two years after the test, in case CFIA inspectors request proof that you performed it. In practice, this means you need a clear mock recall procedure (as part of your preventive control plan) and evidence that you've tested it annually.

Notably, the regulation doesn't prescribe how to conduct the simulation – only that it should be based on your recall procedure and effectively test your ability to recall a food. CFIA expects you to "enable an effective recall" with whatever procedure you use. In other words, your mock recall should mirror a real recall scenario as closely as possible. Industry best practice is to literally simulate a recall event, not just do a paper exercise of tracing product. This means creating a realistic scenario (e.g. discovering an undeclared allergen in a batch, or a contamination issue) and then following your recall plan step by step as if it were real – including assembling your recall team, identifying all affected lots, "notifying" customers, and preparing communications.

The CFIA Recall Procedure Guide emphasizes having a robust recall plan and traceability system in place so that you can rapidly identify affected products and contacts in a recall. CFIA inspectors or auditors may ask to see not only your written recall procedure but also a record or log of any mock recalls or recall drills you've conducted. In an audit, you should be prepared with "a journal or diary of any past recalls, or trial runs of your written procedures". In short, to demonstrate compliance, you need documentation proving that a mock recall was done and showing how it was executed and how effective it was.

Key Elements to Include in a Mock Recall Record

When documenting a mock recall, include comprehensive details so that the record clearly shows what you did and how effective the exercise was. Key elements CFIA would expect in a mock recall record include:

  • Date and Scope of Test: Record the date and time the mock recall was conducted, and a brief scenario description. For example, note if the simulation was based on an allergen issue, contamination, or customer complaint trigger. This sets the context for the exercise. Also note the product involved and reason for recall (e.g. "undeclared milk allergen in Product X, lot 123").
  • Product Identification: Detail the specific product being "recalled" – including product name, size/format, lot code(s) or unique identifier, and any other key descriptors like UPC or production date. The record should show that you can identify the affected lot precisely. Include the quantity of product produced and affected. For example: "Product: Fratello Espresso Blend Coffee, 1 kg bags – Lot #2025-ABC (500 bags produced on Jan 10, 2025)". If multiple lots or products were tested, list each one. This corresponds to what information would appear in a real recall notice (product name, size, codes, etc.).
  • Traceability & Inventory Accounting: Document how you traced the product one step forward and back. This means recording where all the affected product went (distribution) and what remains in your control. Include an accounting of total affected product: e.g. "500 bags produced; 400 distributed to customers; 100 still in warehouse inventory." List the distribution details per lot – essentially a distribution list. CFIA guidance suggests your distribution list should include each recipient's details and the quantity they received. For example, you might tabulate affected shipments: which customers (names and addresses) got the product, on what date, and how much. This shows you can accurately identify the scope of a recall (a core goal of traceability).
  • Recall Team and Roles: Note who participated in the mock recall (your recall management team) and their roles. For instance, list the recall coordinator and team members from QA, operations, sales, etc., with their titles. This demonstrates you assembled the team as per your plan. (You likely have a separate document listing recall team contacts – ensure that is up to date and referenced.)
  • Actions Taken and Timeline: Provide a step-by-step log of what actions were taken during the simulation, and at what times. This is essentially a timeline of the recall drill. It should start from the moment the issue was identified to the final wrap-up of the simulation. Key actions to record include: when the recall was initiated internally, when each team member was alerted, when product traceability information was pulled, when customers would have been notified, etc. Include timestamps to show the speed of your response. For example: "10:00 AM – Recall coordinator notified of issue; 10:15 – Recall team assembled; 10:30 – Pulled distribution records for Lot 2025-ABC; 11:00 – Drafted customer notification," and so on. This timeline helps evaluate whether the recall could be done quickly and effectively. It's also useful to note the person responsible for each action in the record (e.g. "QA Manager retrieved production records; Sales Manager prepared client contact list"). This level of detail shows everyone knew their role and the procedure was followed.
  • Communication and Notification Log: Document all communications made (or that would be made) as part of the recall. This includes internal communications, and simulated external communications:
    • Customer/Client Notification: List the customers or accounts that would be contacted in a real recall, and how you contacted them in the test. For each major customer or distributor, note the contact person's name, the method of contact (e.g. phone, email), and the time contact was initiated. During a mock recall, you should not actually send notices to customers or the CFIA, to avoid confusion. Instead, you can simulate these notifications. For example, you might fill out a draft "Notice of Recall" letter (email) to customers and include it in your records, without actually sending it. CFIA's recall guide even provides a template notice letter (see Annex A) which includes fields for product info, what action the customer should take, and a request for them to acknowledge receipt. Use such a template to craft your mock recall notice. In your record, you can note "Prepared recall notification for Customer X (see Appendix C) – would send via email/phone in real event." Also record any responses if you simulated getting them – e.g. "All three distributors confirmed receipt within 1 hour (simulated)."
    • Regulator Notification: Note when and how you would notify CFIA. In an actual recall, CFIA must be contacted immediately, but in a drill you do not actually contact the CFIA. Instead, document that you "prepared information to notify CFIA recall coordinator" and list what info you gathered for them (such as nature of problem, product details, distribution, etc., which CFIA expects you to have ready). This shows you are prepared to interface with regulators.
    • Public/Media Communication: If your recall plan involves preparing a press release or public notice (for a high-profile recall), note if you drafted one during the simulation. For instance, "Drafted a press release using CFIA's standard template for public warning – not issued (Appendix D)." While smaller recalls may not require this, documenting it in a simulation is beneficial if relevant to your business.
    • It can be helpful to include a Communication Log table in the report for clarity (see template section below). This captures the who, when, how, and outcome for each communication touchpoint.
  • Results and Effectiveness: Summarize the outcome of the mock recall. This section of the record should evaluate how successful the simulation was. Key metrics or results to note:
    • Recall completion time: How long did it take from initiation to having notified all customers and accounted for all product? For example, "All affected clients were identified and (simulated) notified within 3 hours; all product accounted for within 4 hours."
    • Percentage of product accounted for: e.g. "100% of the distributed lot was traced and accounted for during the exercise." In a mock recall, you're aiming to show you could find all product. Regulatory expectation is that you can identify and remove all affected product from sale.
    • Responsiveness of contacts: If you track how quickly each customer responded (even if just simulated), note it. For instance, "Customer A (wholesale) confirmed in 30 minutes; Customer B (retail chain) confirmed in 1 hour," etc., or if any did not respond in the simulation scenario. This highlights any lag in communication.
    • Overall assessment: State whether the mock recall was effective or if gaps were discovered. For example, "Simulation demonstrated we could quickly gather distribution info and communicate with all clients. One outdated phone number was found on the contact list, which has now been updated." This kind of note shows that you are using the exercise to improve.
    • These results address the SFCR requirement to record the "results of the simulation". CFIA and auditors want to see that you analyze how it went, not just list what you did. In fact, guidance suggests asking questions like: "How quickly were you able to conduct the recall? Did team members know their roles? Was all product traced and held? What worked well, and what needs improvement?". Your mock recall record should answer these questions.
  • Follow-Up and Corrective Actions: Finally, include any lessons learned or actions to improve your recall readiness. A mock recall is an opportunity to strengthen your system. Document any issues encountered (e.g. incomplete contact info, delays in obtaining records, confusion about roles) and what you'll do about them. For example: "Noted that lot coding on some bulk bins was not easily cross-referenced to final product – will revise coding system for clarity (Corrective Action by Q2 2025)." Or "Sales team was unsure of who contacts certain regional clients – will update recall team assignments and conduct training." Showing these continuous improvement steps in the record demonstrates to inspectors that the simulation was taken seriously and your recall plan is being refined accordingly.

By including all the above elements, your mock recall documentation will be thorough. Essentially, the record should read like a full report of a recall incident, only it was a test. Anyone reviewing it (internal managers, CFIA, auditors) should be able to understand exactly how the scenario unfolded, how you managed it, and that consumers would have been protected if it were real. Keep the documentation tidy and logically organized with headings and tables, as shown in the template below.

Templates and Guidance Resources

For help in structuring your recall procedures and records, you can draw on several resources:

  • CFIA's Recall Plan Guidance: The CFIA Recall Procedure Guide for food businesses provides a step-by-step outline of what to do in a recall, including preparing distribution lists and recall notices. Annex A of that guide contains a Notice of Recall template – essentially a form letter to inform customers of a recall. This template shows the info you should communicate (product, reason, what the client must do, etc.) and even includes a section for the client to acknowledge receipt. You can adapt this template for your mock recall communications. For example, in a mock recall report, you might include a filled-out copy of this notice as Appendix C (as if it were emailed to customers in the simulation). Using CFIA's template ensures you include all pertinent details.
  • Traceability Tools: CFIA's traceability requirements (SFCR Part 5) underline the importance of maintaining one-step-forward/one-step-back records. Make sure your own traceability documents (purchase records, inventory logs, sales invoices) can quickly produce a list of affected lots and recipients. During a mock recall, you might generate a distribution report from your system – this can be included or referenced in your documentation. For instance, Appendix B of your report could be a printout or spreadsheet of all customers and shipments for the lot in question.
  • Provincial and Industry Templates: Various provincial agencies and industry associations offer sample forms that can be used for recall documentation. For example, Perennia, a food safety agency in Nova Scotia, provides a Mock Recall Record template. Their template instructs businesses to perform the simulation using their actual recall forms (like those for tracking contacts and product) but cautions "DO NOT CONTACT CUSTOMERS NOR CFIA DURING A MOCK RECALL" to prevent false alarms. Instead, it suggests confirming that the contact info you have on file is current. Such templates typically have fields to fill in all the details discussed above (product, lot, date, actions, etc.) in a structured way. They may also include a checklist to ensure you don't forget any step of the recall procedure during the test. You can use these as references to create your own record format that fits your operation.
  • Recall Management Worksheets: In addition to the mock recall record, consider maintaining a Recall Management Log (some templates call it that) which is essentially a contact log for the recall team and external notifications. For instance, a worksheet where you record who on your recall team was contacted, at what time, and when each external party was notified. This can be part of your mock recall report or a supporting document.
  • Internal Templates: If Fratello Coffee Roasters already has a Recall Procedure document (with roles, contact lists, etc.), you should integrate your mock recall reporting with those materials. For example, your recall procedure might have an embedded form or appendix for logging a recall event. Ensure that form captures the required info, or use the template below to supplement it. The goal is that during a real incident or a test, everyone knows which forms to fill out and what information needs to be captured.

Finally, remember to store the completed mock recall records in an organized manner (binder or digital folder), and label them clearly with the date. That way, if CFIA ever inspects your facility or if you're undergoing a food safety audit, you can readily present these records as evidence of recall readiness. 4. Communications and Notifications

We did not actually send this notice during the mock recall, but we simulated emailing it to the three clients listed in the distribution list. We also simulated phone calls to our two largest customers to ensure they received the urgent information (noted at 11:30 and 11:45 in the log). Each client's acknowledgement was documented in the distribution table above (under "Response/Status"). 5. Results of Simulation and Analysis

Overall Outcome: The mock recall was completed (simulation-wise) in approximately 4 hours on the same day. By 13:30, the recall team had identified all affected product and (in simulation) notified all three direct customers carrying that product. 100% of the affected lot was accounted for through inventory and customer confirmations. This meets our recall effectiveness target (account for >99% product within 24 hours).

Effectiveness Evaluation:

  • Speed of Response: The recall team assembled within 10 minutes of initiation and had contacted all clients within 1.5 hours. This is a positive result, indicating a rapid response. We met the CFIA requirement to be able to initiate a recall immediately and inform necessary parties promptly.
  • Team Coordination: All team members knew their responsibilities and followed the recall procedure. The division of tasks (QA handling records, Sales handling customer contact, etc.) worked well. Communication was efficient via the conference call.
  • Traceability: Our traceability system proved robust – we were able to pull up distribution records by lot code in 15 minutes. There were no gaps in the client list; the sum of product on hand and shipped matched the production quantity (500 bags) exactly, which validates our record-keeping.
  • Customer Response: In the simulation, we assumed customers responded promptly. We identified that each major client has a 24-hour contact on file. (During the drill, we also double-checked those contacts. One distributor had an outdated secondary contact email, which we updated immediately.) Obtaining confirmations was simulated, but forms are in place (our notice has a return signature section).
  • Documentation & Communication: All necessary documents (notice, press release, CFIA info) were prepared swiftly. This indicates our recall templates and checklists are effective. Using standardized templates in advance clearly helped expedite the process.
  • Challenges Noted: The mock recall revealed a couple of minor areas for improvement. Firstly, our initial group text missed one team member (the Production Supervisor) because the contact list in the procedure was not updated – he changed his number and we hadn't updated the list. He eventually joined via email. Action: update recall team contact list and ensure it's reviewed quarterly (see follow-up below). Secondly, as noted, one client contact needed updating. No major process issues were encountered.

Overall, the simulation demonstrated that Fratello Coffee Roasters' recall plan is effective, with rapid traceability and communication. We were able to simulate a full recall successfully, meeting CFIA's expectations for recall readiness.

6. Follow-Up Actions (Post-Simulation)

Based on this mock recall, we have identified some follow-up actions to further strengthen our recall preparedness:

  • Update Contact Lists: Immediately update the Recall Team contact list (Appendix F in our full Recall Procedure manual) with the new phone number for the Production Supervisor. Going forward, institute a quarterly verification of all team contact info. Likewise, verify the key account contacts list every 6 months (Sales dept. to lead) so customer details are always current.
  • Refine Training: Provide a short refresher training to the recall team on the recall procedure, highlighting the timeline from this drill. Emphasize the importance of each step and maintaining composure during the process. Especially ensure backups are aware of their roles in case primary team members are unavailable.
  • Improve Documentation Process: Although the documentation in this exercise was adequate, we will create a pre-formatted "Recall Timeline Log" form to use during an actual recall. This will make it even easier to jot down times and actions in the moment (to avoid any omissions in a real crisis). We'll add this log template as an appendix to our Recall Procedure.
  • Supplier Communication Plan: Since this scenario was internal, we didn't need supplier info. In a future drill, we may simulate a scenario requiring notifying a supplier (for example, if an ingredient was the cause). We plan to run a different mock recall next year focusing on upstream communication (supplier notification) to test that aspect.
  • Continuous Improvement: Document these improvements in our Preventive Control Plan. We will review and approve the changes in the next management meeting. The learnings from each mock recall will be folded into our food safety system. As required, this mock recall report will be kept on file (hard copy and electronic) for at least 2 years. It will be available for CFIA inspection to demonstrate compliance and proactive food safety management.
