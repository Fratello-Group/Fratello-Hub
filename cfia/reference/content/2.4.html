1. Purpose

To provide a standardized method for sourcing, receiving, analyzing, and grading green coffee, ensuring quality, traceability, and food safety compliance with CFIA's Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR). This SOP integrates with Cropster and other internal systems.

2. Scope

Applies to all green coffee samples received from importers, including sample evaluation, lot tracking, storage, roasting for assessment, and traceability setup.

3. Responsibilities

RoleResponsibility
Head Roaster / QAOversees evaluations and final approvals
Green Coffee TeamConducts intake, grading, and documentation
Warehouse TeamHandles physical receipt and storage of coffee

4. Frequency

As Needed – Whenever new green coffee samples are received or full shipments arrive.

5. Working with Coffee Importers

Identify reputable importers (e.g., Royal Coffee, InterAmerican, Atlas Coffee Importers). Request sample lots with full details:

  • Origin, Variety, Process Method, Lot #, Q Grader Score, Certifications
  • Track samples in transit; record upon arrival using Cropster Sample Management.

Green Coffee Importer Food Safety Expectations

All green coffee importers Fratello works with must demonstrate clear and documented food safety practices that align with CFIA's SFCR standards and hazard control requirements. These include the following practices:

  • Importers must source only from FDA-registered or equivalent suppliers who are reviewed annually.
  • Each lot of coffee must be evaluated prior to shipment and upon arrival for moisture, defects, and sensory quality.
  • Moisture readings must be performed using validated equipment (e.g., SHORE® 930 Moisture Meter) with a target of ≤13%.
  • Bags should be labeled with "For Further Preparation Only" to reflect that coffee is not ready-to-eat.
  • If risk factors are identified, importers must have access to LAAF-accredited third-party labs for mycotoxin testing.
  • Suppliers should have documented GMPs and follow Foreign Supplier Verification Programs (FSVP) or equivalent standards.
  • All findings, including sampling and verification activities, should be logged and traceable to the specific lot number.

These requirements ensure preventive controls are in place across the supply chain and that any upstream hazards (e.g., mold, mycotoxins) are monitored and mitigated prior to Fratello's roasting process.

This policy supports compliance with SFCR Section 89 and provides assurance during CFIA inspections that hazard controls are implemented at the import stage and downstream at Fratello's roasting operations, under SFCR Section 89 (Preventive Controls) and supports our downstream hazard management strategy.

6. Receiving and Storing Samples

  • Log the following for each sample:
    • Importer Name
    • Lot Number (as assigned by exporter)
    • Origin, Variety, Process Method
    • Date Received
  • Store samples in a cool, dry space:
    • Temperature: < 25°C
    • Relative Humidity: < 65%
    • Off the floor (minimum 4") and away from light
    • Pest-free, sanitary conditions

7. Green Quality Analysis

  • Sample Size:
    • 300g for specialty lots, 100g for lower-grade lots
  • Defect Grading:
    • Use SCA Green Grading Classification
    • Primary defects (e.g., full black beans, sour): None allowed
    • Secondary defects: ≤ 5 per sample
  • Moisture Content:
    • Acceptable Range: 9–13% (measured via moisture meter)
  • Color, Odor, Uniformity: Visually inspect on black grading mat with 4000K+ lighting
  • Optional: Screen size breakdown and density check
  • Record results in Cropster Sample or Grading Log

8. Sample Roasting

  • Roast samples using the Roest sample roaster (50g–200g capacity)
  • Choose/create profile based on origin and process method
  • Allow beans to rest 8–24 hours post-roast before cupping
  • Ensure roaster is cleaned between samples to prevent cross-contamination

9. Cup Quality Evaluation

For cupping setup and scoring, refer to SOP: FCR-QA-CUP001 - Cupping Protocol & Quality Evaluation.

Summary of Process:

  • Use SCA-standard ratio, temperature, and timing
  • Score fragrance, aroma, flavor, acidity, body, aftertaste, uniformity, balance, sweetness, overall
  • Enter all data into Cropster QC module
  • Minimum Score to Approve: ≥80 (Specialty Grade)

10. Purchase Approval

  • If coffee meets grading and cupping benchmarks, initiate purchase:
    • Confirm importer documentation and lot ID
    • Log sample decision in Cropster
    • Record purchase terms and approval reason

11. Receiving Full Shipment

  • Inspect packaging for damage, verify lot number and quantity
  • Store green coffee in warehouse under approved conditions
  • Log inventory in Cropster and internal system

12. Re-Sampling for QA Verification

  • Pull representative sample (multiple bags) from shipment
  • Repeat Green Grading and Cupping Process (as above)
  • Compare to original results
  • If discrepancies found:
    • Hold product from production
    • Notify supplier for corrective action

13. Traceability and Lot Tracking

  • Use exporter-assigned Lot Number throughout lifecycle
  • Cropster Integration:
    • Sample > Green Inventory > Roast Batch > "Roasted On" Packaging Date
    • Lot # becomes Roast ID during production, then "Roasted On" date on consumer packaging
  • Maintain linkage between Lot # and Roasted Product in Cropster

CFIA Compliance Note: This process satisfies SFCR Part 5 traceability by maintaining chain-of-custody records from supplier to final packaged good.

14. Food Safety Considerations

  • Verify lot numbers against documentation
  • Clean all equipment prior to use
  • Store coffee off the floor and away from moisture/light
  • Use clean containers for cupping and roasting
  • Document and act on any non-conformance

15. Recordkeeping & Training

  • All records (grading logs, cupping scores, approvals) must be maintained for minimum 12 months
  • Staff are to be trained annually or when procedures are updated

Process Breakdown & Detailed Guidance

This section provides a breakdown of the process for working with coffee importers, receiving and evaluating samples, roasting, analyzing quality, purchasing, receiving orders, re-sampling, and tracking lot numbers, tailored for Fratello Coffee Roasters' food safety manual. It incorporates industry standards and best practices, ensuring both quality control and food safety considerations, with a focus on compliance with the Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA) regulations.

Working with Coffee Importers

The initial step involves establishing relationships with coffee importers to source high-quality green coffee. Start by researching and identifying reputable importers such as Royal Coffee or InterAmerican, focusing on those offering the desired coffee types (e.g., specific origins, varieties, processing methods). Contact these importers via email or phone, providing details about Fratello's requirements, such as quality standards and volume needs. Request information about available lots, which typically includes origin, variety, processing method, price, and any pre-existing quality scores from Q graders or other certifications.

Review the importers' catalogues or listings to identify lots that align with Fratello's criteria. Select lots for further evaluation and request samples, ensuring the importers understand that these samples are for quality assessment. Track the delivery of samples, using tracking numbers provided by the courier, and log their arrival to maintain a record. This step is crucial for ensuring a smooth supply chain and setting expectations for quality and consistency.

For food safety, ensure importers comply with CFIA regulations, particularly the Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR), which require importers to have a preventive control plan (PCP) and provide necessary documentation. Verify that the coffee is labelled "For Further Preparation Only" if exempt from certain import requirements, as outlined in CFIA Food Import Regulations.

Receiving and Storing Samples

Upon receipt, log each sample with relevant details, including the importer's name, lot number, origin, variety, processing method, and date received. This log should be maintained in a digital database or spreadsheet for easy access and traceability. Store the samples in a cool, dry place, ideally at a temperature below 25°C and humidity below 65%, to prevent moisture absorption and mold growth, which are critical food safety concerns. Ensure storage areas are clean, pest-free, and away from light to maintain coffee quality.

Research suggests that green coffee can last up to a year if stored properly, away from light, heat, and air, as noted in Klatch Coffee Storage Best Practices. Best practices include keeping coffee off the floor (minimum 4 inches), maintaining clean pallets, and ensuring at least 20 inches between coffee piles, as recommended by the Green Coffee Association, detailed in Royal Coffee Storage Solutions. For food safety, regularly inspect storage areas for pests and contamination, aligning with CFIA guidelines for safe food handling.

Green Quality Analysis

Green quality analysis involves a physical inspection of the green coffee beans to evaluate their suitability for roasting, using the Specialty Coffee Association of America (SCAA) green coffee classification system. Begin by preparing a representative sample, weighing out 100g for lower-quality lots or 300g for high-grade coffee with few expected defects, as per SCAA guidelines (SCAA Green Coffee Grading). If necessary, sort the beans by size using screens (e.g., 14/64" to 18/64" diameter holes) to separate different sizes, recording the percentage in each screen.

Inspect the sample on a flat, well-lit surface, identifying and counting defects. SCAA categorizes defects into primary (e.g., black beans, sour beans, insect-damaged beans) and secondary (e.g., broken beans, faded beans, quakers), with specific equivalents for each (e.g., one full black bean equals one primary defect). Record the number of each type, calculating defects per kilogram to determine the grade. Measure moisture content using a moisture meter, ensuring it falls within 9-13% for specialty grade, and evaluate color and uniformity for additional quality indicators.

This step is vital for identifying physical issues that could affect cup quality, such as mold or insect damage, which pose food safety risks. Ensure all equipment used (e.g., screens, scales) is clean and sanitized to prevent cross-contamination, aligning with CFIA's emphasis on preventive controls.

Roasting Samples on Roest Sample Roaster

Roasting is performed using the Roest sample roaster, a professional machine designed for small batches, with capacities ranging from 50-200g per batch (ROEST Sample Roasters). Set up the roaster by ensuring it is properly connected to power and, if applicable, WiFi for data logging. Calibrate temperature probes if required, following the manufacturer's instructions, to ensure accuracy.

Weigh out the desired amount of green coffee, ensuring it is clean and free from debris. Select or create a roasting profile based on the coffee's characteristics, such as origin and processing method, using the roaster's digital interface. Load the sample into the roaster, start the process, and monitor it, adjusting parameters if in manual mode. The Roest roaster features first crack detection and dual fan systems for cooling, allowing back-to-back roasting without daily limits. Once roasting is complete, cool the beans using the roaster's cooling system and package them in airtight containers to preserve freshness, minimizing exposure to air and potential contamination.

This step is critical for evaluating cup quality, and food safety considerations include ensuring the roaster is regularly cleaned to prevent residue buildup, which could affect subsequent batches. The roasting process also helps kill any microorganisms present, aligning with CFIA's focus on thermal processing as a control measure.

Analyzing Cup Quality Using Q Grade Standards

After roasting, analyze the cup quality using Q grade standards, which involve a standardized cupping procedure outlined by the Specialty Coffee Association (SCA). Grind the roasted coffee to a medium-fine consistency suitable for brewing, using a clean grinder to avoid cross-contamination. Set up a cupping station with clean, odor-free equipment, including cupping bowls, spoons, and hot water at 92-96°C.

Follow the SCA cupping protocol: measure 8-10 grams of ground coffee per 150 ml of water, pour hot water over the grounds, and wait 4 minutes to break the crust with a spoon, releasing aromas. Wait an additional 6-10 minutes for the coffee to cool to a suitable tasting temperature, then evaluate by slurping to assess fragrance, aroma, flavor, acidity, body, and aftertaste, using the SCA's official cupping form (SCA Coffee Cupping Protocol).

Score each attribute on a 10-point scale, summing up to a total out of 100, with scores above 80 indicating specialty grade.

This sensory evaluation is essential for final quality judgment, and food safety considerations include ensuring all cupping equipment is sanitized and the environment is free from odors that could skew results, complying with CFIA's hygiene standards.

Purchasing the Sample

Based on the combined results of green quality analysis and cup quality score, decide whether to purchase the lot. If the coffee meets Fratello's quality standards (e.g., no primary defects, cup score ≥80), place the order with the importer, specifying the lot number, quantity, and any contractual terms for quality assurance. Ensure the purchase agreement includes provisions for lot tracking and re-sampling upon receipt, aligning with food safety and quality control practices.

For food safety, ensure the agreement holds the supplier accountable for any safety issues, and verify that the importer provides traceability information, as required by CFIA under the SFCR.

Receiving the Order into the Warehouse

Upon arrival, inspect the shipment for any damage or discrepancies, verifying the lot number and quantity against the order. Store the coffee in a controlled environment, maintaining temperature and humidity levels to prevent spoilage, and ensure the warehouse is clean and pest-free. Log the receipt in the inventory system, linking it to the original sample's quality data for traceability.

Research suggests that storage conditions should be cool and dry, with temperatures between 60-70°F, as noted in Cafe Imports Storage Best Practices. For food safety, comply with CFIA guidelines for warehouse hygiene and pest control, ensuring regular monitoring to prevent contamination.

Re-Sampling the Product to Ensure Quality

To verify that the received coffee matches the approved sample, take a representative sample using proper sampling techniques, such as taking subsamples from multiple bags. Repeat the green quality analysis and cup evaluation as described above, comparing results with the original sample. If significant differences are found (e.g., increased defects, lower cup score), investigate with the supplier, potentially rejecting the lot if food safety or quality standards are not met.

This step is crucial for food safety, as discrepancies could indicate contamination or mishandling during transport. Maintain records of re-sampling and any actions taken, aligning with CFIA's traceability requirements.

Tracking Lot Number of the Bag to Ensure Consistency

At Fratello Coffee Roasters, lot numbers are not assigned internally but are instead provided by coffee exporters from regions around the world. Each lot number is pre-printed on the coffee sacks shipped to us, assigned by the exporting country and company. This lot number encapsulates critical specifications about the coffee, including its origin, variety, processing method, and other vital details relevant to the purchase.

Upon receiving the coffee sacks, record the lot number as printed, along with additional details such as origin, variety, processing method, quality scores, and receipt date, in a digital database or spreadsheet for initial documentation. This lot number is then entered into Cropster, the roaster software used by Fratello, to begin the tracking process. In Cropster, the lot number serves as the primary identifier during the sample evaluation and quality assurance stages, linking directly to the green coffee inventory once approved.

After completing a final production roast, the lot number in Cropster is transformed into a roast number. This roast number uniquely identifies the specific batch roasted from that lot, capturing details such as roast profile and date. During the packaging process, the roast number is further transitioned into a "Roasted On" date, which is printed on the finished coffee bags. This date serves as the final traceable marker for the product, enabling Fratello to monitor consistency and quality through to the consumer.

This tracking system ensures full traceability from the exporter-provided lot number to the packaged product, which is crucial for food safety and regulatory compliance. The lot number connects the coffee back to its source, allowing identification of the exporting country and company if issues arise. The roast number and subsequent "Roasted On" date provide precise production tracking, facilitating rapid response to potential recall issues involving tainted products or damaged goods. By maintaining detailed records at each stage—lot number in Cropster, roast number post-production, and "Roasted On" date on packaging—Fratello ensures compliance with CFIA regulations, particularly the Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR), which mandate traceability and documentation for imported foods.

Food Safety Considerations

  • Accurate Entry: Verify the lot number on the sacks matches the importer's documentation before entering it into Cropster to prevent tracking errors.
  • Record Keeping: Maintain comprehensive records in Cropster and supplementary databases, linking lot numbers to roast numbers and "Roasted On" dates, for audit readiness.
  • Traceability: Use the lot number to trace coffee back to its exporter and origin, and the "Roasted On" date to identify specific batches shipped to customers, ensuring swift action in case of safety concerns.

This process leverages the pre-assigned lot number from exporters, integrates it seamlessly into Cropster, and transitions it through production stages, ensuring consistency and safety from receipt to retail.

Employees will be retrained on procedures as required.
